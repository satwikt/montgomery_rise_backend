DOCUMENT: Montgomery Alabama — Community and Economic Context
PARCEL_ID: general
TOPIC: community_overview

Montgomery is the capital city of Alabama with a population of approximately 200,000. It is the birthplace of the American civil rights movement — Rosa Parks refused to give up her bus seat here on December 1, 1955, and the Montgomery Bus Boycott that followed became a turning point in US history. The city owns more than 200 vacant parcels across its footprint, many concentrated in historically underinvested neighbourhoods on the west and south sides. RISE was built to help the City of Montgomery convert idle public assets into community opportunity, guided by real data — ArcGIS foot traffic, FEMA flood risk, 311 distress signals, and active federal grant windows.

DOCUMENT: Montgomery — Civil Rights Heritage Economy
PARCEL_ID: general
TOPIC: heritage_economy

Montgomery's civil rights heritage is a growing economic driver, not only a historical fact. Key landmarks and their significance:

Rosa Parks Museum, 252 Montgomery Street. Operated by Troy University. Commemorates Rosa Parks' arrest and the Montgomery Bus Boycott. Receives approximately 8,420 documented visits annually tracked in the city's Most Visited Locations ArcGIS dataset.

The Legacy Museum, 115 Coosa Street. Operated by the Equal Justice Initiative (EJI). Documents the history of slavery, racial terror, and mass incarceration in America. Receives approximately 6,250 documented visits. EJI, founded by Bryan Stevenson, has invested over $50 million in Montgomery's heritage corridor.

Civil Rights Memorial Center, 400 Washington Avenue. Operated by the Southern Poverty Law Center. Receives approximately 5,100 documented visits. Features the Civil Rights Memorial designed by Maya Lin.

Dexter Avenue King Memorial Baptist Church, 454 Dexter Avenue. Where Martin Luther King Jr. pastored during the Bus Boycott. A National Historic Landmark.

Heritage tourism pattern: ArcGIS foot traffic shows the heritage corridor is active on weekends, with Saturday and Sunday peaks. Visitors travel from across the country and internationally. Parcels A and B are both within 0.35 miles of multiple heritage landmarks — they are in the middle of a growing destination zone that lacks supporting community amenities.

EJI's ongoing investment: The National Memorial for Peace and Justice (the lynching memorial) opened in 2018 and has drawn over 500,000 visitors. EJI's investment has transformed downtown Montgomery's heritage economy. Parcels near the EJI corridor have rising, not declining, development potential.

DOCUMENT: Montgomery — Food Desert Crisis in 36108
PARCEL_ID: general
TOPIC: food_desert_context

The USDA Economic Research Service defines a food desert as a low-income census tract where a substantial number of residents have low access to a supermarket — in urban areas, more than 500 residents or 33% of the census tract population live more than 1 mile from a supermarket.

Montgomery's confirmed food desert: ZIP code 36108, the Maxwell Heights / Gibbs Village neighbourhood where Parcel C is located at 1600 Terminal Road, is formally designated by the USDA as a food desert. Both criteria are met: poverty rate 35% (above the 20% threshold) and nearest full grocery (Piggly Wiggly / Food Outlet on West Fairview Avenue) is 1.4 miles away.

Key statistics for the Parcel C zone (ACS 2023 and PIH 2024):
- Nearest full grocery: 1.4 miles. Without a car and on Montgomery's 60-minute bus schedule, this is effectively inaccessible for routine shopping.
- Food insecurity: 32.0% of residents.
- Poverty rate: 35.0%.
- Median household income: $29,400.
- Chronic disease burden: elevated rates of asthma, diabetes, hypertension, and heart disease confirmed by PIH 2024.
- Transportation barrier: confirmed as the root cause of food inequity in this zone. theM bus routes run every 60 minutes — a round-trip grocery trip without a car requires 3–4 hours.

What a community grocery co-op is: A member-owned grocery store where community residents are shareholders. Co-ops are proven models for bringing grocery access to food deserts because they are community-accountable — profit stays in the neighbourhood, and the store is governed by its members. Examples: Seward Community Co-op in Minneapolis, New Roots Co-op in St. Louis.

DOCUMENT: Montgomery — MGMix and Digital Infrastructure Economy
PARCEL_ID: general
TOPIC: tech_economy

MGMix is the Montgomery Metropolitan Internet Exchange — a facility where internet service providers and large technology companies connect their networks locally. Data generated and consumed in Montgomery does not route through Atlanta or Birmingham. MGMix has attracted data infrastructure investment from AWS, Google, and Meta to the Montgomery metro area. Meta has committed $1.5 billion for a 1.3 million square foot data centre approximately 2 miles east of Parcel B.

Why MGMix matters for RISE: When a large technology company builds data infrastructure near an IX node, it creates local demand for network operations centre (NOC) technicians, cybersecurity analysts, IT support and systems administration, and data centre infrastructure maintenance staff. These roles pay $45,000–$85,000 annually and require 6–18 months of technical training rather than a four-year degree. Parcels near MGMix are RISE's top candidates for workforce training that pipelines locally trained workers into high-paying technology roles.

Parcel B at 0.214 miles from MGMix is the closest hero parcel to the IX node, making it the strongest candidate for a technology workforce training hub.

DOCUMENT: Montgomery — Maxwell AFB and Veteran Workforce
PARCEL_ID: general
TOPIC: maxwell_afb

Maxwell Air Force Base is located in Montgomery and employs approximately 14,500 military and civilian personnel. It is the home of Air University — the Air Force's graduate-level professional military education system — and hosts numerous defence technology programmes.

Maxwell AFB's relevance to RISE: Veterans transitioning out of Maxwell AFB carry technical skills in communications, cybersecurity, logistics, and systems management that translate directly into technology sector roles. They represent a high-potential workforce for defence contracting and technology employment. A training hub or co-working campus near Maxwell AFB — such as Parcel B on Upper Wetumpka Road at 2.1 miles — provides a natural transition pathway.

SBA programmes: The SBA SBIR DoD Phase I grant (open, 52 days remaining) covers 100% of qualifying costs for defence-adjacent and veteran-owned businesses with no match required. Veteran-owned small businesses at a Parcel B co-working campus are natural applicants.
